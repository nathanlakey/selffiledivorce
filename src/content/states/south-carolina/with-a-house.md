# South Carolina Divorce With a House — Your Options (2026)

The family home is typically the most valuable marital asset in a South Carolina divorce. Because SC is an equitable distribution state with fault as a factor, both the overall fairness of the division and any marital misconduct can affect how home equity is allocated.

---

## Is the Home Marital Property?

**Purchased during the marriage with marital income:** Marital property — subject to equitable distribution.

**Owned before the marriage by one spouse:** Non-marital property — typically stays with original owner. However:
- Mortgage payments from marital income during the marriage may create a marital interest in the equity
- Placing the home in joint names during the marriage may convert it to marital property

**Inherited home:** Non-marital property — stays with inheriting spouse. Keep documentation.

**Purchased after formal separation began:** May be treated as non-marital property (acquired after the separation date).

---

## Option 1 — One Spouse Keeps the Home

**Settlement Agreement must include:**
- Full address and legal description
- Agreed fair market value (professional appraisal or agreed estimate)
- Equity calculation: agreed value − mortgage payoff − closing costs = net equity
- Each spouse's share of net equity (equitable — document the basis, including any fault consideration)
- Buyout method: cash, offset against retirement or other marital assets
- **Mandatory refinancing deadline** — keeping spouse refinances into their name alone within X days of Decree
- **Fallback provision:** If refinancing fails, the home is listed for sale
- Who pays mortgage, taxes, insurance, and HOA during the transition period
- **Deed transfer:** Leaving spouse signs Quit Claim Deed after refinancing → record at county Register of Deeds

---

### Recording the Deed in South Carolina

After the Decree and refinancing:
1. Prepare a **Quit Claim Deed** (standard for divorce transfers in SC)
2. Leaving spouse signs and notarizes
3. Record at the **County Register of Deeds** in the county where the property is located
4. Pay recording fees ($15–$50)
5. South Carolina does not impose a deed recording tax for transfers incident to divorce — confirm with the Register of Deeds

---

## Option 2 — Sell and Split Proceeds

**Settlement Agreement must include:**
- Each spouse's percentage of net proceeds (equitable — document basis)
- Listing timeline after Decree
- Agent selection method
- Who pays carrying costs during listing
- Who occupies and whether occupancy compensation applies
- Price reduction triggers and frequency
- What happens if either party refuses to sign closing documents

---

## Option 3 — Deferred Sale (Often Used with Children)

One spouse stays in the home — typically the custodial parent — for a defined period, then the home is sold.

**Settlement Agreement must include:**
- Duration of deferral (specific date, event, or condition — e.g., youngest child graduates high school)
- Who pays all carrying costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance, HOA, maintenance)
- Occupancy compensation to the vacating spouse (or explicit waiver)
- Capital improvement rules and cost sharing
- Sale process and net proceeds split at end of deferral
- Refinancing requirement (vacating spouse off the mortgage within X months)
- Default provisions if conditions are not met

---

## Fault Consideration in Home Equity Division

Because fault is Factor 15 in South Carolina's equitable distribution, marital misconduct can affect the home equity division:
- If one spouse committed adultery and it is proven, the innocent spouse may receive a larger share of the equity
- Courts can award the family home or a larger equity share to the innocent spouse

Document any fault issue carefully — it can have real financial consequences.
